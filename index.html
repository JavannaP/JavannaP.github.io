16 Shots and a Conviction

By Javanna Plummer

October 9, 2018 6:00 PM

The Chicago Police Department (CPD) has a history of controversy, so when new situations arise, they only intensify deep-seated issues. In August, the Chicago Police Department sparked public outcry in the Southside Englewood community after partnering with Norfolk Railroad Service to stage a “bait truck.” This is what community members called an unmarked truck full of Nike gym shoes, which the CPD parked in Englewood. According to Police Supt. Eddie Johnson this was a measure to lure potential thieves, CNN reported.

Afterward, a viral video gained over 600,000 shares and caught the attention of rapper Vic Mensa, who staged an “anti-bait truck” event giving out free shoes. Mensa has been a public critic of the CPD and even clashed with CPD officers at the recent Bud Billiken Parade, the nation’s largest African American parade. While serving as Grand Marshall, Mensa was accompanied by protesters who held up a sign saying: 'Convict Jason Van Dyke.'

Jason Van Dyke is a Chicago Police Officer who was just convicted of 2nd degree murder and 16 counts of aggravated battery for killing Laquan McDonald on October 20, 2014.

This case also gained considerable attention and moved the United States Department of Justice (DOJ) to probe into the CPD’s questionable tactics. In a 164-page report republished by VOX, the DOJ investigated the CPD’s “abusive behavior.”

The DOJ wrote: 'Black youth told us that they are routinely called pieces of shit’ by CPD officers. A 19-year-old black male reported that CPD officers called him a ‘monkey.’ Such statements were confirmed by CPD officers.'

The DOJ was not the only one moved to action by McDonald’s death. Independent journalist Brandon Smith partnered with Chicago activist William Calloway to force the CPD to release the dashcam footage. Smith filed a Freedom of Information Act request (FOIA). When a citizen makes this request, record holders are given deadlines on when they are due to respond.

After repeatedly asking for extensions, the CPD eventually denied Smith’s request. A judge had to force the CPD to release the tapes, which led to a peaceful demonstration on Michigan Avenue and the DOJ investigation.

Seemingly, the CPD considered the impact that the release of the tapes had on Chicago, especially on Black Chicago. Before the verdict was read on Friday, the Chicago Sun-times reports that the CPD deployed over 4,000 officers to various neighborhoods in anticipation of riots if Van Dyke was acquitted. Given other high-profile acquittals, such as when Officer Darren Wilson was acquitted after killing Michael Brown, there was an expectation of an acquittal, which, for the CPD, seemed to also accompany an expectation of violence.

Nonetheless, Rev. Marvin Hunter, McDonald’s uncle who was present throughout the trial, made it known that he was not in support of violence. 'We’re trying to set precedent here,' he noted in a press conference.

Calloway, who held a prayer vigil on the first day of arguments, called for peace as well. Calloway attended the entire trial and has been instrumental in seeking justice for McDonald. In their '16 shots' podcast, which closely follows the trial, WBEZ reports that Calloway was visibly surprised when Van Dyke was found guilty and Calloway lost his balance at one point. When he was approached by the sheriff, Calloway regained his composure.

However, Calloway did not hide his opinion on the verdict. Upon leaving the courthouse, he captivated bystanders when he xclaimed, 'Everything changes after today!'

Calloway was speaking directly to nearby officers, and he was not the only one who felt like this trial will set a precedent for police conduct.

'I’m hoping that it opens a lot of eyes in terms of the police treating us fairly, and maybe they’ll think twice about being so quick to pull the trigger when it comes to African Americans in the city of Chicago,' said Rena Shepherd.

To Calloway and many, Black Chicago was on the stand demanding justice for McDonald. 'Today, Black Chicago won,' Calloway said.
